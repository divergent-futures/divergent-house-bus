## 1. The gap in the market (grounded)
Mass-market RVs are **poorly built and fail predictably**: industry data has **water intrusion as the #1 cause of used-RV problems**, alongside wall/roof **delamination**, electrical failures, cabinetry loosening, and appliances failing early — a 30-year dealer called current quality *"some of the worst stuff I've seen."* Meanwhile the buyer base is huge and growing: **50+ are the RV industry's core segment** (boomers ~22–26% of the market), millennials/Gen Z are now ~22% (a historic inflection), full-timers are increasingly driven by housing costs, and the US RV market is ~**$36B → ~$53B by 2030** (~8% CAGR).

**So the opportunity is a vehicle that is durable, safe, low-hassle, and cheap to run — exactly what RVs fail at — offered across price points.** That's what the tier line does.

## 2. Why this beats a traditional RV (the design answer)
| RV failure (industry) | House BUS answer |
|---|---|
| Water ingress (#1), roof/window/slide leaks | **Sealed structural composite monocoque** — the pack *is* the floor; no stick-and-staple frame to delaminate or rot |
| Wall/roof delamination, soft floors | Composite sandwich body + structural pack; no wood underlayment to swell |
| Electrical failures, propane risk | **All-DC**, fewer failure points, **no propane**; point-of-use, documented |
| Fire risk | **Non-flammable sodium** chemistry + real crash structure (rocker, crush zones) |
| Expensive to run / hook-up hungry | **Solar + high efficiency** → a **30-amp hookup is plenty**; drive-the-edges-of-the-day |
| Un-repairable, dealer-dependent | **Open-source + repairable** — parts and build documented; a co-op or owner can fix it |

## 3. Personas by tier
### ENTRY (~$22k pack · ~110 mi hwy / ~165 local)
- **Margaret & Ron, 66 — retired, cost-first.** Summer near a national park, winter near the grandkids; **slow seasonal migration**; 30-amp hookups; want zero-hassle, safe, no RV rot. *The huge, underserved boomer market* — simple lives, one or a couple, value reliability over range. 165 local miles easily reaches the park; then they plug in.
- **Dave, 52 — solo trade contractor.** Lives in it near job sites; **reliability + low running cost** matter more than range; a mobile home + base that never leaks.
- *(also: housing-priced-out singles wanting a durable, cheap, moving home.)*

### MID (~$39k pack · ~217 mi hwy / ~321 local)
- **Priya, 34 — content creator / remote worker.** Moves **state-to-state slowly**, works online; wants comfort + enough regional range. **The sweet spot for most buyers.**
- **Priced-out couple, 30s.** Housing-cost refugees who want a real home that moves — *built like a vehicle, not a shed* — for materially less than a house.

### HIGH (~$55k pack · ~307 mi hwy / ~450 local)
- **The Hendersons, 58 — full-time adventurers (means).** Cross-country; **tow the toad** into tight parks; boondock far off-grid for weeks. The **full-thesis reference build**.
- **Off-grid family.** Needs the capacity + solar to live off-grid with kids; range + autonomy over price.

## 4. The strategic point
**One open-source platform, three cell fills.** A builder, co-op, or owner assembles the tier that fits the customer — the **same safe, sealed, efficient, repairable bus at every price point**. The Entry tier is the wedge into the biggest, most under-served, most reliability-hungry segment (retirees); the High tier is the halo/reference build; the Mid tier is the volume default. Open-sourcing the whole thing means the design serves people we'll never manufacture for.
